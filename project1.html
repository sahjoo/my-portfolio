Overview

Problem

Smartphones give everyone the tools to take stunning photos, but they have brought about concerns as well, such as depletion of storage space, loss of files, and security concerns. There are few photo storage services that offer a good balance of file sharing and security features.

Solution

The answer was to create fun service that helps the everyday photo taker to store, organize, collaborate, and share their media files while also offering controlled security and privacy. Our service needed to help users store their photos and videos securely to the cloud, and and make organizing, sharing and collaborating easy and enjoyable.



Research

User Surveys

First, user data was needed to figure out what type of cloud storage services users use, why they are used, what is lacking, and other helpful info to determine what problem(s) needed to be solved. The following findings from the user survey were most notable:

  • • 100% access service on browser
  • • 79% use cloud storage for personal use
  • • 54% dissatisfied with organization, security/privacy, or file sharing
  • • 100% save documents, spreadsheets and photos

These findings told us where the focus should be:

  1. 1. Organization 
  2. 2. Privacy and Security 
  3. 3. File Sharing and Collaboration

Competitive Analysis

After performing a SWOT analysis on popular cloud storage services like Dropbox and Google Drive, it was clear the market was overflowing with file storage apps, but only a few photo storage apps. I saw an opportunity to create something for our users in this market. Upon analysis, Google Photos, Flickr, and Cluster have uses for certain audiences, but none of them solved the major problems identified in the survey for our target audience.

User Personas

In order to focus on the motivations, goals and frustrations of our potential users, I created two user personas to bring the voice of my users to life. Since they were most concerned about organization, privacy/security, file sharing and collaboration, I made these features important to the personas as well.

For the first part of the design phase, I sketched and digitally created low fidelity wireframes. Then I conducted usability testing to ensure easy readability and navigation. We only encountered out issues with consistency in the writing and the small search bar size. I made adjustments based on this feedback. Branding

The first draft of the brand name and logo sketches were for Snaphero, with the concept of the “snaps” being rescued by the product. However, the glyphs in the logo letters were too small when rendered digitally. The comic style that I wanted to display for the website design started to take too much time also.

As a result, I created alternative logos (Squirrel and Photostache) and shifted the brand style to a rustic or hipster vibe.

After all, I do live in hipster city. The concept was that users could stash their photos and videos using the service. Through preference testing, Photostache (#5 to be exact) won potential users over with its obvious meaning and punny humor.

From there, I put together a moodboard and refered to that to create a brand style guide.

Hi-Fi Mockups

I incorporated images and the brand styles into the wireframes to create high fidelity mockups. I used preference testing again to help determine the best design options.

This time I conducted usability testing on the mockup prototype. There was one consistent issue that was raised by all participants. The more options icons needed labels for those who are not familiar with them.

Being close to age 60, one of the participants couldn’t complete some of the tasks because she didn’t know what the icons meant.

Finally, I added hovering labels to the icons and other changes, which lead us to the final iteration of the product.

Conclusion

What I learned

Looking back, I think perhaps too much time was spent pondering on design decisions when preference tests could’ve been utilized early on. I made many personal assumptions that were proven wrong after listening to my test participants (like when I forgot to show icon labels). This was the one time in my life I enjoyed being proven wrong because it made me improve the design. Also, I learned that encountering problems early in the design phase is necessary and saves so much time and headache down the line.

What I would change

Even though my research data suggested that users like to access their storage mostly on their desktop, I would've liked to spend more time refining the mobile version because I know that accessibilty on multiple devices is very appealing right now. And to stay on brand, I would've loved to make this app more fun and enjoyable for users!
